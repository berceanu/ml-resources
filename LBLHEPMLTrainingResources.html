The Hitchhiker's Guide to Machine Learning:

ML for itinerant physicists who just need to get to their destination. Bring a towel.

How to read (and edit) this document:

DON’T PANIC! This compiled list of ML resources may seem overwhelming, but it’s organized so that you can easily find material on specific topics or you can follow the quick-start guide for a suggested way to get started if you’re totally new to ML.

Resources are organized by topic for targeted material (e.g. a blog post on CNNs or a presentation on sequential models) and by resource type (e.g. book, online course) for compilations that cover many topics.

Formatting:
Because the goals of ML in physics don’t always neatly map onto what industry leaders are doing (and by far the majority of ML resources come from industry), physics-specific resources are highlighted. The type of resource is indicated in brackets:

  • [Tu] = Tutorial/code snippet
  • [Bl] = Blog
  • [Sl] = Slides (lectures, presentations)
  • [Cs] = Course
  • [Bk] = Book
  • [Pa] = Paper

Quick-start guide:

Existing books and online courses may not generally be the most efficient way to learn modern ML practice - some context and basics from presentations plus interactive tutorials/code snippets that show exactly how to implement the ideas covered will do the job much faster. One can always explore specific ideas/applications as they become relevant! The underlying theory can be interesting, but is most useful if you’re trying to develop entirely new ML algorithms - generally the CS experts have done a much better job of this (though this may be changing now). Of course, your mileage may vary. If you’ve found a different approach works better, we’d all love to hear your experience!

“I want to start from the beginning.”

  • Check out one of the quick intro to ML presentation sets: either NERSC’s Deep Learning for Science 2019 intro presentations or (slightly) more physics-oriented intro presentations from Sergey Gleyzer (collider physicist)
  • Run through the Intro to ML tutorial from the DANCE-ML 2020 workshop; consider watching the related video linked there for further context
  • Read Andrej Karpathy’s blog post on a pragmatic approach to deep learning here (Scott K endorses this; some of the more technical suggestions are specific to image processing). The punchline:
  • “A “fast and furious” approach to training neural networks does not work and only leads to suffering… The qualities that in my experience correlate most strongly to success in deep learning are patience and attention to detail… I have developed a specific process for myself that I follow when applying a neural net to a new problem… [which] builds from simple to complex and at every step of the way we make concrete hypotheses about what will happen and then either validate them with an experiment or investigate until we find some issue. What we try to prevent very hard is the introduction of a lot of “unverified” complexity at once, which is bound to introduce bugs/misconfigurations that will take forever to find (if ever).”
  • Optional: Read the intro to Deep Learning, section 1.2 for a brief history of the field
  • Optional: Read Contemporary ML for Physicists up through section 1.9 for a seat-of-the-pants intro to some basic common knowledge in the field from a physicists’ perspective

Books [Bk]:

  • Contemporary ML for Physicists (Jared Kaplan; theoretical physicist, OpenAI researcher) - focused on neural nets specifically, and generally deep learning; more colloquial and easy-to-read (aside from a very information-theory-heavy section 2 and the start of section 3) than some other books here, making it more suitable to read through full sections (rather than just as a reference); perhaps still less immediately practical than tutorials or slides, but good for going a bit deeper. A suitable quick substitute for an intro to ML CS course like the Stanford or Google ones above.
  • Deep Learning - includes a number of more advanced topics; still more theoretical and less focused on implementation, but good as a reference and to fill in gaps that quick tutorials/slides can’t cover
  • Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning (Bishop, 2006) - good reference for ML theory; won’t include some of the latest
  • An Introduction to Statistical Learning (2015) by Gareth James, Daniela Witten, Trevor Hastie and Robert Tibshirani - You can download this introductory book pdf directly or go to this webpage for more sample codes and datasets in R. Some sample codes on python will be posted later. Similar content to the Stanford intro ML course (perhaps a bit more theory-oriented than ideal, not much on deep learning).
  • The Elements of Statistical Learning (2008) by Trevor Hastie, Robert Tibshirani, Jerome Friedman. This is a slightly more advanced book with more in-depth discussions on topics like neural networks and support vector machines. You can download the pdf directly or go to this link for more sample codes and datasets in R.
  • Neural Networks and Deep Learning - An online textbook by Michael Nielsen focusing on neural networks.
  • ML in astronomy website - Online textbook plus Python code examples focused on astronomy

Papers [Pa]:

  • A Living Review of ML for Particle Physics - links to all sorts of ML in physics papers, organized by topic, including review papers. Largely collider physics + neutrino detectors, but that’s also representative of the literature.
